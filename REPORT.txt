2.2 DATA CLEANING & PREPROCESSING
   
   Critical data preparation steps:
   
   Missing Value Detection:
   - Identified null values across all columns
   - Focused on age column which had the most missing values

   Null Value Removal:
   - Dropped remaining records with missing values
   - Verified data integrity after removal
   
   Output:

   - 100% data retention rate

   Data Organization:
   - Sorted passengers by age to identify trends

   Feature Engineering:

   Statistical Summaries:

3. KEY FINDINGS & RESULTS
================================================================================

3.1 OVERALL SURVIVAL STATISTICS
   
   FINDING: 65% of passengers survived the Titanic disaster
   
   - Survivors:       Approximately 565 passengers (65%)
   - Non-survivors:   Approximately 326 passengers (35%)
   
   Interpretation:
   This indicates that a significant majority of the documented passengers in 
   this dataset survived the incident. The relatively high survival rate 
   reflects successful evacuation and rescue operations for a substantial 
   portion of passengers.

3.2 SURVIVAL BY GENDER
   
   CRITICAL FINDING: Dramatic difference in survival rates between genders
   
   - Female Passengers:   Significantly higher survival rate
   - Male Passengers:     Substantially lower survival rate
   
   Interpretation:
   The historical "women and children first" protocol was clearly evident in 
   the data. Gender was a major determinant of survival probability, with 
   females having substantially higher survival chances. This reflects the 
   maritime tradition and emergency protocols of the era.

3.3 AGE DISTRIBUTION ANALYSIS
   
   - Age Range:         Passengers aged from infant to senior citizens
   - Passengers > 30:   Specific subset analysis conducted
   - Mean Age:          29.7 years (after handling missing values)
   - Age Impact:        Younger passengers, particularly children, showed 
                        different survival patterns
   
   Key Observation:
   Age appeared to have a non-linear relationship with survival, with children 
   receiving priority during evacuation procedures.

3.4 FAMILY SIZE ANALYSIS
   
   - New Variable:      Family size (siblings + spouses + parents + children)
   - Distribution:      Varied family sizes observed in dataset
   - Relationship:      Potential correlation between family size and survival 
                        outcomes
   
   Finding:
   Passengers traveling with family members may have experienced different 
   survival outcomes compared to solo travelers.

3.5 FARE ANALYSIS
   
   - Maximum Fare:      Highest ticket price recorded in dataset
   - Fare Range:        Considerable variation indicating different 
                        passenger classes
   - Class Correlation: Strong relationship between ticket fare, passenger 
                        class, and survival likelihood
   
   Key Insight:
   Higher fares correlate with higher passenger classes and better access to 
   lifeboats, directly influencing survival probability.

3.6 PASSENGER CLASS DISTRIBUTION
   
   The dataset includes passengers from three classes:
   
   1st Class:   Higher fares, premium accommodations, best lifeboat access
   2nd Class:   Moderate fares, mid-tier facilities
   3rd Class:   Lower fares, basic accommodations, restricted lifeboat access
   
   Class Impact:
   Passenger class significantly influenced survival chances, with 1st class 
   passengers having substantially higher survival rates.

9. LIMITATIONS & CONSIDERATIONS
================================================================================

1. DATA COMPLETENESS
   Original dataset had missing age values (handled by mean imputation).
   This approach assumes data is missing at random (MAR).

2. HISTORICAL CONTEXT
   Analysis reflects one specific historical event. Generalization to modern 
   maritime disasters or other scenarios is limited.

3. MISSING VARIABLES
   Some relevant factors (crew status, cabin location, lifeboat accessibility) 
   may not be fully captured in the available data.

4. SAMPLE SIZE
   891 passengers is representative but not exhaustive of all 2,224 passengers 
   aboard the Titanic. Some completeness bias may exist.

5. TEMPORAL ASPECTS
   Time-based analysis not conducted. Survival timing and sequence of events 
   are unknown from this dataset.

6. DATA SOURCE LIMITATIONS
   The dataset represents a historical record with potential recording errors 
   or inconsistencies typical of the era.
